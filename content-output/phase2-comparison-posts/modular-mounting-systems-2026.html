What Is a Modular Mounting System?

A modular mounting system separates a mount into discrete components, each serving a specific function:

  • Base: The part that attaches to the surface (suction cup, magnetic base, drill plate, post clamp, seat rail clamp)
  • Arm/Extension: The connecting piece that provides reach and positioning (short arm, long arm, flexible arm, fixed angle)
  • Ball joint: The pivot mechanism that allows rotation and angle adjustment (typically 25mm or 17mm balls)
  • Holder/Cradle: The part that grips the device (universal tablet holder, phone holder, scanner cradle, device-specific cradle)
  • Adapters: Components that bridge between different sizes or systems (ball size adapters, AMPS plate adapters)

Each component connects to the next through a standardized interface (usually a ball-and-socket joint). This means any base can connect to any arm, any arm can connect to any holder, and you can mix and match to build exactly what you need.

Why Modularity Matters: 5 Real Scenarios

1. You upgrade your tablet

With a one-piece mount, a new tablet often means a new mount. With a modular system, you swap just the holder ($20 to $40) and keep the base and arm you already have installed.

2. You move the mount to a different vehicle or location

A suction-base mount does not work on a forklift. A drill-base mount does not transfer to a new truck. With modular mounting, you swap the base component and keep everything else. Moving from a truck windshield to a forklift post? Change the base, keep the arm and holder.

3. You need to add a barcode scanner

Rather than buying a separate mounting system for the scanner, you add a scanner holder to your existing base or post. iBOLT's 38mm post system supports both tablet and scanner holders on the same mounting point.

4. You need a longer or shorter arm

The mount reaches too far, or not far enough. With a one-piece mount, you are stuck. With a modular system, you swap the arm for a different length. iBOLT offers arms from 2 inches to over 6 inches.

5. You deploy across a mixed fleet

Your warehouse has Toyota forklifts, Crown forklifts, and Hyster forklifts. Each has different mounting points. With modular mounting, you use the same holders and arms everywhere, and swap only the base to match each forklift model. One inventory of parts covers the whole fleet.

When One-Piece Mounts Make More Sense

Modular is not always the right answer. One-piece mounts work well when:

  • Your setup will never change. If you know the exact device, exact vehicle, and exact position, and none of those will change for years, a purpose-built one-piece mount is simpler.
  • You want zero assembly. Some users prefer to open a box and have a complete mount ready to go. iBOLT sells pre-assembled configurations for common setups, but if you build your own from components, there is some assembly involved.
  • You need ultra-compact design. Modular joints add a small amount of bulk at each connection point. For extremely tight spaces, a one-piece solution might fit where a modular one does not.
